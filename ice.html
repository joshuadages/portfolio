THE INSTITUTE
OF CULINARY EDUCATION
Full Rebrand
Apr–Nov 2018 | Parsons | Adobe Ai, Ps, Id
A rebranding project completed as an assignment for Parsons School of Design. I chose to rebrand The Institute of Culinary Education as my subject.
ICON and LOGO
Throughout my logo reworking process, I chose to focus on “The Institute” as a nickname and move toward a logo more representative of the culinary arts.
BUSINESS CARDS
The two main designs for the Institute are the employee business card and the promotional card used to distribute at career fairs, culinary events, etc.
DIGITAL
The Institute’s website and mobile app use an image carousel of slides on the home page promoting upcoming events. Some slides will contain static images, while others will allow for looped videos.
PRINT
A brochure promoting a multi–night event that brings three of the world’s best pastry chefs to the New York campus.
PROMOTIONAL
The new branding can be used on large banners outside of The Institute’s downtown New York City building, as well as in their new Los Angeles location. This display reaches potential students and informs the general public of events that are open to everyone.
PROCESS
In its existing branding, The Institute of Culinary Education colloquially shortens its name to “ICE.” The logo’s red square and colored stripes do not directly reference cooking or education.
My redesign process started with adding utensils into the logo, moving from a square to a diamond, and then working with medallions and ribbons, each recognizable symbols in the culinary world.
I determined that a simple letter and knife shape would encapsulate everything that represents The Institute.
BRAND STYLE GUIDE OVERVIEW
Using the clean but classic style of the Marion typeface hearkens back to the roots of The Institute’s founder, Peter Kump, while the more modern Mr Eaves Mod OT type shows The Institute’s dedication to moving forward.

The new color palatte is more natural and appetizing in its hues, solidifying The Institute’s leading spot in the industry.
